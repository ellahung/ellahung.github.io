Service Design Case Study

Nottingham Adult Social Care “Front Door”

Understanding and improving how people first access adult social care support.

Project Overview

Making the “front door” clearer and more user-centred

This project focused on the initial access “front door” of Nottingham’s adult social care service. Our team worked with staff and stakeholders to understand how people currently enter the service, where they get lost in the system, and how we might make the first contact clearer, more consistent, and more user-centred.

Duration

3 months
Service design studio

Deliverables

Interviews, CJM, stakeholder map, blueprint, concepts & vision

My Role

Service designer – research, synthesis, mapping, alignment

Context & Challenge

Why this “front door” matters

Nottingham’s adult social care “front door” involves multiple entry points, channels and teams. For people seeking support, it can be hard to know where to start, what information they need to provide and what will happen next. For staff, fragmented processes and different mental models of the service make collaboration and handovers more difficult.

Our challenge was to build an accurate “as-is” picture of the front door, identify pain points and root causes rather than just symptoms (such as “too many calls”), and outline a future-state vision that could make access clearer, more equitable and more sustainable for both users and staff.

Team & My Role

How I contributed

We were a small team of service design students working closely with tutors and a client representative from adult social care.

  • Planned and conducted conversations and interviews with frontline staff and managers.
  • Turned qualitative stories into front-stage/back-stage customer journey maps, a stakeholder map and a service blueprint.
  • Helped facilitate discussions to clarify the problem statement and align on which parts of the front door to focus on first.
Research Approach

How we understood the service

We combined qualitative research methods with service design tools to understand both the user-facing experience and the internal service reality.

Staff interviews

Spoke with frontline staff and managers about how enquiries arrive, how they are handled, and what makes cases straightforward or complex. We treated interviews as structured conversations, paying attention to both what people said and how they behaved.

Onsite observations

Observed how enquiries were triaged and handed over between teams, noting workarounds, breakdowns in communication and time spent redirecting “misrouted” cases. This helped us see where the journey fragmented for both users and staff.

Desk & secondary research

Reviewed public-facing information and internal documents, and carried out secondary research on demographics, online search behaviour and emotional experience to narrow down potential user archetypes.

We used service mapping, front-stage/back-stage journey maps, and stakeholder/service provider mapping (with colour coding) to build a rich “as-is” picture. Later, hypothesis statements, 5W1H and an impact–effort matrix helped us structure ideation around the most critical pain points instead of jumping straight into random solutions.

Insights

What we discovered

Unclear starting points

People often did not know which channel or team to contact first, leading to repeated explanations and longer resolution times.

Internal vs user language

Team names and internal terminology made sense to staff but were confusing for people using the service for the first time.

Fragmented mental models

Different teams held slightly different versions of “how the front door works”, which made collaboration and handovers harder.

Time spent redirecting

Staff spent significant time redirecting enquiries that were “sent to the wrong place”, reducing capacity for more complex cases.

Beyond “too many calls”

Demand and pressure were also driven by unclear expectations, fragmented information and unclear roles across different teams, not just phone volume.

Visualising the system

Journey map & service blueprint

Current journey map

We mapped the end-to-end journey from first enquiry through triage and handover, highlighting key touchpoints, channels and pain points for people seeking support. The journey map helped us see where information was repeated, lost or delayed.

Service blueprint

The service blueprint connected front-stage interactions with backstage processes, support systems and teams. It became a shared artefact that helped stakeholders discuss the service at a system level rather than only from the perspective of individual teams.

Concept Directions

Where we decided to focus

Based on the insights and blueprint, we explored several concept directions instead of committing to a single solution. I contributed to shaping and grounding these concepts in what we heard from staff.

Awareness campaigns (TV/radio)

TV and radio adverts to help people understand what adult social care can and cannot provide before they call, better aligning expectations with the actual scope of the service and reducing avoidable demand.

AI bot for quicker information searching

A conversational AI bot to help people find the right information or contact option more quickly, reducing misrouted enquiries and repeated explanations, and easing pressure on front-line staff.

Self-service portal for transparency

A simple self-service portal where people can check where they are in the process and what will happen next, making the waiting period more transparent and reducing anxiety.

Together, these directions were intended as a future-state vision: a combination of clearer information, more transparent processes and better alignment between user expectations and service capacity.

Outcomes & Learning

What changed & what I learned

This project helped our client see the front door as a whole system and helped me clarify the kind of work I enjoy most as a service designer.

Outcomes
  • The journey map and service blueprint provided a visual “big picture” that stakeholders could react to, making discussions more concrete and focused.
  • The work helped reveal where different teams’ mental models aligned or diverged, and which parts of the front door were most urgent to address.
  • Our concept directions opened up conversations about how digital tools, communication and service processes could work together to reduce avoidable demand.
What I learned
  • Visualising a complex service is a powerful way to align people who each see only one part of the system.
  • Spending time on a solid “as-is” picture and structured ideation helps avoid rushing into solutions that do not match real pain points.
  • I learned a lot about collaboration in cross-cultural teams and the importance of asking earlier, speaking up for myself and keeping professionalism even after disagreements.
  • This project confirmed that I enjoy talking to people, listening to different perspectives and helping teams turn messy, real-world stories into shared understanding and actionable directions.
