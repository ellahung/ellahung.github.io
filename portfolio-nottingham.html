Service Design Case Study

Nottingham Adult Social Care “Front Door”

Understanding and improving how people first access adult social care support.

Project Overview

Making the “front door” clearer and more user-centred

Focused on improving the “front door” of Nottingham’s adult social care service by identifying breakdowns in user entry and first-contact clarity.

Duration

3 months
Service design studio

Deliverables

Interviews, CJM, stakeholder map, blueprint, concepts & vision

My Role

Service designer – research, synthesis, mapping, alignment

Context & Challenge

Why this “front door” matters

Nottingham’s adult social care “front door” spans multiple entry points and teams, creating uncertainty for users and fragmentation in staff processes and understanding.

We built an “as-is” view of the front door to identify root causes beyond surface symptoms and define a clearer, more equitable, and more sustainable future-state.

Team & My Role

How I contributed

As part of a small service design team, I worked closely with tutors and the client to understand the current service and shape future improvements.

  • Conducted interviews with frontline staff and managers.
  • Synthesised research into service journeys and key insights.
  • Facilitated discussions to define the problem space and prioritise improvement opportunities.
Research Approach

How we understood the service

We combined qualitative research methods with service design tools to understand both the user-facing experience and the internal service reality.

Staff interviews

Interviewed frontline staff and managers to understand enquiry handling, decision-making, and operational challenges, capturing both reported experiences and observed behaviours.

Onsite observations

Observed enquiry triage and cross-team handovers, uncovering communication gaps, workarounds, and delays caused by misrouted cases, highlighting fragmentation across the end-to-end journey.

Desk & secondary research

Reviewed public and internal data alongside secondary research to define user archetypes.

Mapped the current service ecosystem and prioritised key opportunities using hypothesis statements, 5W1H, and an impact–effort matrix.

Insights

What we discovered

Unclear starting points

People often did not know which channel or team to contact first, leading to repeated explanations and longer resolution times.

Internal vs user language

Team names and internal terminology made sense to staff but were confusing for people using the service for the first time.

Fragmented mental models

Different teams held slightly different versions of “how the front door works”, which made collaboration and handovers harder.

Time spent redirecting

Staff spent significant time redirecting enquiries that were “sent to the wrong place”, reducing capacity for more complex cases.

Beyond “too many calls”

Demand and pressure were also driven by unclear expectations, fragmented information and unclear roles across different teams, not just phone volume.

Visualising the system

Journey map & service blueprint

Current journey map

We mapped the end-to-end journey from first enquiry through triage and handover, highlighting key touchpoints, channels and pain points for people seeking support. The journey map helped us see where information was repeated, lost or delayed.

Service blueprint

The service blueprint connected front-stage interactions with backstage processes, support systems and teams.
It became a shared artefact that helped stakeholders discuss the service at a system level rather than only from the perspective of individual teams.

Concept Directions

Where we decided to focus

Based on the insights and blueprint, we explored several concept directions instead of committing to a single solution. I contributed to shaping and grounding these concepts in what we heard from staff.

Awareness campaigns (TV/radio)

TV and radio adverts to help people understand what adult social care can and cannot provide before they call, better aligning expectations with the actual scope of the service and reducing avoidable demand.

AI bot for quicker information searching

A conversational AI bot to help people find the right information or contact option more quickly, reducing misrouted enquiries and repeated explanations, and easing pressure on front-line staff.

Self-service portal for transparency

A simple self-service portal where people can check where they are in the process and what will happen next, making the waiting period more transparent and reducing anxiety.

Together, these directions were intended as a future-state vision:
A combination of clearer information, more transparent processes and better alignment between user expectations and service capacity.

Outcomes & Learning

What changed & what I learned

This project helped our client see the front door as a whole system and helped me clarify the kind of work I enjoy most as a service designer.

Outcomes
  • Created a shared view of the current service through journey maps and service blueprints, enabling more focused discussions with stakeholders.
  • Identified key gaps in team understanding and prioritised opportunities to improve the front door experience.
  • Developed future-state concepts that explored how digital tools, communication, and service processes could work together.
Key learning
  • Service visualisation is a powerful way to align stakeholders around complex systems.
  • Investing time in understanding the current state leads to better prioritisation and more effective solutions.
  • This project strengthened my skills in user research, service design, and facilitating cross-functional collaboration.
