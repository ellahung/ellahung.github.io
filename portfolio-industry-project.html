Industry collaboration case study

Sky Sports – Women’s Football Experience

Exploring how to make women’s football easier to find, follow and care about on a major sports platform.

Duration
4 weeks
Deliverables
Current journey, fan insights, concept flows, low-fi prototypes
Role
UX / Service Designer
Project overview

Making women’s football more visible, social and easy to follow

In this four-week collaboration with Sky Sports, our team explored how to improve the digital experience around women’s football. We focused on three everyday fan problems: keeping track of matches in busy calendars, watching together with friends even when they cannot meet in person, and giving women’s football more equitable visibility alongside the men’s game within the Sky Sports app.

Sky Sports covers a wide range of sports, leagues and competitions. While coverage of women’s football has grown, fans still told the client that it felt harder to find, follow and return to compared with the men’s game. Our goal was to focus on a realistic slice of this challenge: the everyday digital experience of a fan who wants to keep up with women’s football, from finding live games and highlights to discovering stories and players they care about.

Team & My Role

Working with Sky Sports

We were a small team of UX and service design students, working with tutors and Sky Sports representatives who joined our check-ins to share feedback and constraints.

  • Helped shape our focus around realistic journeys rather than trying to fix everything about women’s sport.
  • Mapped how fans currently navigate to women’s football content across the app and website.
  • Led the creation of user flows and wireframes to communicate ideas clearly in review sessions.
  • Co-presented our final concepts, explaining trade-offs between fan needs and business priorities.
Process

How we approached the brief

With four weeks and limited direct access to fans, we combined client sessions, light fan research and journey mapping to move from a broad topic to three concrete experience improvements.

Step 01
Client sessions

We started with briefing and Q&A sessions with the Sky Sports team to understand their goals around women’s football, current coverage and technical constraints. This helped us narrow the scope to journeys that could realistically be affected in the near term.

Step 02
Fan journeys & scenarios

We mapped an end-to-end journey before, during and after the match for women’s football fans, including searching for match information, adding matches to calendars, watching live or on catch-up and staying engaged through news and discussions.

To bring this to life, we storyboarded scenarios such as Vivian adding a match to her calendar and receiving a reminder, or Nick noticing a women’s match while checking men’s results and deciding to watch.

Step 03
Ideation & prototyping

Based on these journeys, we clustered opportunity areas and prioritised them using an impact-effort lens. We then developed low-fidelity storyboards and a clickable prototype that addressed three key experience gaps: scheduling, social watching and equitable visibility for women’s football.

Insights

What we discovered

Matches are easy to forget

Fans often noticed women’s fixtures but did not have a simple way to add them into their everyday calendars, which meant matches were easily forgotten or clashed with other plans.

Watching together is hard to organise

People wanted to share matches with friends and family, but childcare, travel and distance made physical watch-parties difficult, especially for women’s fixtures.

Visibility is uneven

Men’s results and stories were more prominent by default; women’s football content was present but less central, so fans had to work harder to notice and follow it.

Journeys are fragmented

Fans stitched together their experience across apps, social media and TV, which made it harder to maintain a consistent relationship with women’s football on Sky.

Small changes can shift perception

Equal placement for men’s and women’s teams, plus simple prompts and reminders, could help normalise women’s football as an integral part of the club ecosystem rather than an add-on.

Solution snapshots

Key features we proposed

Our final concept combined three features: streamlining match scheduling, enabling remote watch-parties, and giving women’s football more equitable visibility next to the men’s game.

01
Equitable visibility for women’s football

The “My Team” and news views present men’s and women’s teams side by side, showing both results and upcoming fixtures in one place. This normalises women’s football as part of the same club ecosystem and encourages fans to engage with both.

02
Streamlining match scheduling

Fans can add women’s (and men’s) matches directly from the Sky Sports app into their personal calendar with a single tap, then receive timely reminders on match day so they do not miss games they care about.

03
Watching together from anywhere

A “Live Chat Room” feature lets fans invite friends to watch the same match and talk together in a shared space, even when they cannot meet in person, turning women’s fixtures into more social moments.

Prototype

Bringing the flows to life

We built a mobile prototype in Figma to demonstrate the key flows: checking “My Team” for women’s fixtures, adding a match to the calendar, joining a live chat room and watching in a private room with friends.

In our final presentation to Sky Sports, we used the prototype to walk through the experience from a fan’s point of view and discuss how these ideas could fit into their roadmap.

01

Fans start from “My Team” to see both men’s and women’s fixtures in one place.

02

From a selected match, they can add it directly to their personal calendar.

03

When the game starts, they join a live chat room to watch and react with friends.

04

For smaller groups, a private room keeps the conversation focused on their own circle.

User Testing

Quick user feedback

Calendar felt the most useful

Participants saw the “add to calendar” feature as the most immediately helpful part of the concept.

“I literally forget the kick-off times sometimes... this also creates a sense of anticipation!” P01, P02

Smaller live chat rooms felt more comfortable

People preferred watching matches with a small, close group rather than a large public room.

“I prefer to enjoy the game with the people closest to me, like my family or friends.” P01, P02

Equal space for women’s football was appreciated

Participants were glad to see women’s football content given equal space to men’s on the “My Team” page, making it feel less like an add-on and more like a default part of the club experience.

Reward system moved to a later phase

We decided to move the reward system and physical events into a later phase, as they would resonate more with users who had already become women’s football fans.

“I think the game-watching invite is enough.”
“But I’d love to see warm-up activities before the season! We don’t have anything like that right now.” P01, P02

Future

Three years plan

Year 1 – Strengthen the core experience

Focus on the “My Team” calendar, visibility of women’s fixtures, and small-group live chat as the default way to watch together.

Year 2 – Introduce a light reward system

Experiment with simple in-app rewards linked to watching matches, interacting in chat, and inviting friends.

Year 3 – Connect rewards to real-world events

Gradually add physical rewards and events that target users who have already become regular women’s football fans.

Outcomes

What this collaboration gave us

For Sky, our work provided a clearer picture of how women’s football currently appears in their digital experience and a set of concrete ideas that could be explored further. For me, it was a chance to connect my interest in women’s sport with practical UX and service design work.

  • Delivered an as-is set of fan journeys and experience maps focused on women’s football discovery.
  • Provided user flows and wireframes that made it easier to discuss trade-offs between visibility, space and complexity.
  • Received encouraging feedback from the client about the clarity of our storytelling in the final presentation.
Learning

What I learned

This project helped me practise turning a broad cultural issue into a focused product opportunity.

  • How to quickly align a design team around a focused slice of a broad, emotionally charged topic like women’s sport.
  • The importance of balancing ambition with what can realistically be implemented in the short term.
  • How to use journeys, flows and low-fi prototypes to turn abstract goals like visibility into specific interaction changes.
  • That I am particularly motivated by projects that mix media, sport and representation, and I enjoy turning that passion into structured, evidence-based design work.
